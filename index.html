Why numbers instead of chord names?

Both Roman numerals and Nashville numbers describe a chord by its scale degree — its position in the key — instead of its letter name. The payoff is that a progression becomes transposable: 1–4–5 is C–F–G in the key of C, G–C–D in the key of G, E♭–A♭–B♭ in E♭. Learn the numbers once and you know the progression in every key. This is how Nashville session players read charts and how musicians call tunes on the bandstand.

Roman numerals (the classroom system)

Quality is shown by letter case: uppercase is major (I, IV, V), lowercase is minor (ii, iii, vi), and the diminished chord gets a degree sign (vii°). In C major: I ii iii IV V vi vii° = C Dm Em F G Am Bdim.

Nashville numbers (the working system)

Plain Arabic numbers, always counted from the major scale of the key. Major is unmarked; minor gets a dash (6- is the vi chord); diminished gets °; sevenths are added as superscripts (5⁷ dominant, 1Δ⁷ major 7, 2-⁷ minor 7). In C major: 1 2- 3- 4 5 6- 7°.

In a minor key, Nashville charts keep numbering from the parallel major, so the naturally lowered degrees are written with flats: the C minor progression Cm–A♭–E♭–B♭ is charted 1- ♭6 ♭3 ♭7.

How to practice: switch Notation to Nashville (names hidden) — the chart shows only numbers, and the current chord's name is revealed as it plays. Then change the key and notice the numbers don't change. When you can hear "1 5 6- 4" coming, you own the progression in all twelve keys.
